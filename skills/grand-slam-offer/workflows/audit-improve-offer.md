<objective>

Deliver brutal, Alex Hormozi-style critique of an existing offer, then provide specific, actionable fixes. No softening. The goal is a better offer, not comfort.

**Input:** User's current offer (however they describe it)
**Output:** Honest assessment + prioritized improvement plan

</objective>

<intake>

Ask: "Share your current offer. Include as much as you can:
- Who is it for?
- What's the main promise/outcome?
- What's included?
- What's the price?
- Any bonuses, guarantees, deadlines?
- How are you currently positioning/selling it?"

**If they give sparse details:** Push back. "I need more to give you useful feedback. What exactly does someone GET when they buy? What do you SAY to sell it?"

</intake>

<process>

## Step 1: First Impression Hit

Before analyzing systematically, give your gut reaction. Be direct.

"Here's my first impression: [Honest reaction]"

Examples of honest first impressions:
- "This sounds like every other [category] out there. Why would someone choose YOU?"
- "I don't know what I'm actually getting. The promise is vague."
- "The price is way too low for what you're offering. You're leaving money on the table."
- "This is a feature list, not an outcome. No one buys features."
- "Who is this even for? 'Business owners' isn't specific enough."

Don't soften it. They came for feedback, give it to them.

## Step 2: Value Equation Scorecard

Score each driver 1-10:

| Driver | Score | Evidence | Problem |
|--------|-------|----------|---------|
| Dream Outcome | ?/10 | [What they promise] | [Why it's weak/strong] |
| Perceived Likelihood | ?/10 | [Proof elements] | [Why they might not believe] |
| Time Delay | ?/10 | [Speed of results] | [Why too slow] |
| Effort & Sacrifice | ?/10 | [What customer must do] | [Why too hard] |

**Scoring guide:**
- 9-10: Exceptional. Hard to improve.
- 7-8: Good. Minor improvements possible.
- 5-6: Average. Needs work.
- 3-4: Weak. Major gap.
- 1-2: Critical failure. This is why the offer isn't working.

**Call out the lowest score:** "Your biggest problem is [lowest score driver]. This is likely why [consequence]."

## Step 3: Grand Slam Components Audit

Check for presence AND quality of each component:

| Component | Present? | Quality | Issue |
|-----------|----------|---------|-------|
| Validated Market | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| Clear Dream Outcome | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| Problem-Solution Stack | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| Optimized Delivery | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| Scarcity | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| Urgency | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| Bonuses | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| Guarantee | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| M.A.G.I.C. Name | ? | ? | [Be specific] |
| Premium Price | ? | ? | [Be specific] |

**Be brutal on quality:**
- "You have bonuses but they're weak. 'Access to community' isn't a bonus, it's table stakes."
- "Your guarantee is buried and timid. It should be front and center, bold."
- "This isn't scarcity, this is fake urgency. 'Limited time' means nothing if the timer resets."

## Step 4: The Killer Questions

Ask these directly. Demand answers.

**Differentiation:**
- "If I removed your company name, could I put any competitor's name on this offer and it would still make sense?"
- "What can a customer get from you that they literally cannot get anywhere else?"

**Belief:**
- "Why should someone believe this will work for THEM specifically?"
- "What proof do you have that this delivers the promised outcome?"

**Value:**
- "Would YOU pay this price for this offer? Be honest."
- "What would need to change for you to 10x the price?"

**Clarity:**
- "If someone asked 'what do I get?' can they answer in one sentence?"
- "After hearing this offer, does someone know EXACTLY what to do next?"

## Step 5: Prioritized Fix List

Based on the audit, create a ranked list of improvements:

```
PRIORITY FIXES (Do These First)

1. [Most critical issue]
   - Current: [What they have now]
   - Problem: [Why it's hurting them]
   - Fix: [Specific action to take]
   - Impact: [Expected improvement]

2. [Second most critical]
   - Current: ...
   - Problem: ...
   - Fix: ...
   - Impact: ...

3. [Third most critical]
   - Current: ...
   - Problem: ...
   - Fix: ...
   - Impact: ...

SECONDARY FIXES (After Priority Fixes Done)

4-N. [Additional improvements]
```

**Prioritization logic:**
1. Lowest Value Equation score first
2. Missing Grand Slam components second
3. Quality improvements third

## Step 6: Quick Win Identification

Find 1-2 things they can fix TODAY:

"Here's what you can change in the next hour that will improve this offer:
1. [Quick win 1]
2. [Quick win 2]"

Quick wins are usually:
- Sharpen the headline/name
- Add a specific deadline
- State a guarantee boldly
- Remove wishy-washy language
- Add a specific number/metric

## Step 7: Before/After Preview

For the top 3 fixes, show what "after" looks like:

```
FIX #1: [Issue]

BEFORE:
[Their current version]

AFTER:
[Improved version]

WHY IT'S BETTER:
[Specific reason tied to Value Equation]
```

## Step 8: Implementation Check

Ask: "Which of these fixes do you want to work on now?"

Options:
1. Work through fixes one by one
2. Focus on quick wins only
3. Take the report and implement yourself
4. Get specific help on one area (scarcity, guarantee, naming, etc.)

If they want to work through fixes, route to appropriate section:
- Market issue → references/market-selection.md concepts
- Delivery issue → references/offer-creation-process.md
- Enhancement issue → specific enhancement reference
- Naming issue → references/naming-magic.md

</process>

<brutal_feedback_examples>

Use these as calibration for tone:

**On vague outcomes:**
"'Transform your business' - what does that even mean? That's not an outcome, that's a bumper sticker. What SPECIFICALLY changes? Revenue? Time? Stress? Give me a number and a timeframe or this means nothing."

**On weak guarantees:**
"'Satisfaction guaranteed' is the most meaningless phrase in marketing. Everyone says it, no one believes it. What SPECIFICALLY are you guaranteeing? Results? Timeline? What happens if they don't get it?"

**On fake scarcity:**
"'Limited spots available' - how many? Why? When do they run out? If you can't answer these questions, the scarcity isn't real and sophisticated buyers will smell it."

**On commodity positioning:**
"You're selling [category]. So are 10,000 other people. Why YOU? 'Because I care about results' isn't a differentiator - everyone says that. What's your unfair advantage?"

**On low pricing:**
"You're charging $X for something that could transform [outcome]. Either you don't believe in your own offer, or you're trying to compete on price. Both are losing strategies."

**On missing proof:**
"I see claims but no proof. No testimonials, no case studies, no numbers. Why should anyone believe this works? 'Trust me' isn't marketing, it's hoping."

</brutal_feedback_examples>

<when_they_push_back>

If they defend a weak element:

"I hear you, but let me push back: [specific counter]. The question isn't whether YOU believe it's fine - it's whether your TARGET CUSTOMER is convinced. Are they buying? If not, something isn't working."

If they say "but that's how everyone does it":

"Exactly. And how's that working for 'everyone'? You're not trying to be average. A Grand Slam Offer is specifically designed to be UN-comparable. 'Everyone does it' is an argument AGAINST doing it."

If they resist a specific improvement:

"Okay. I've told you what I see. You can proceed without addressing [issue], but know that you're leaving [specific consequence] on the table. Your call."

Don't argue endlessly. Give the feedback, make the case once, respect their decision.

</when_they_push_back>

<success_criteria>

Audit & Improve is complete when:
- [ ] First impression delivered (honest, direct)
- [ ] Value Equation scored with specific evidence
- [ ] All Grand Slam components audited
- [ ] Killer questions asked and answered
- [ ] Prioritized fix list created
- [ ] Quick wins identified
- [ ] Before/after shown for top fixes
- [ ] User has clear next action (implement themselves or work through fixes)

</success_criteria>
